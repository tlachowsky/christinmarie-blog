The Teacher Supplies I Buy With My Own Money (And Why I Don't Mind)

Affiliate disclosure: This post contains affiliate links. If you purchase through my links I may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. See my full disclosure policy.

Every August, without fail, someone in my life who is not a teacher finds out that I spend my own money on classroom supplies and reacts like I've just told them I enjoy doing taxes for fun.

"They don't give you that stuff?" No, not all of it. "Don't you get a stipend?" Yes, a small one. "And you still spend more?" Yes. Every year. Happily.

Here's the thing: my classroom is where I spend a huge chunk of my waking life. The kids who come through my door deserve a space that works -- that's organized, that has the supplies they need, that feels like somewhere worth showing up to. That matters to me more than keeping a few extra dollars in my pocket. So yes, I spend my own money. I've made my peace with it.

What I have NOT made my peace with is spending more than I have to. Which is why almost everything on this list came from Walmart.

The Things I Buy Every Year No Matter What

Dry erase markers. I go through these like water. The ones the school provides are fine but I have a brand I love and I just buy them myself. A big pack from Walmart lasts me most of the year.

Sticky notes. In every size. I use them for everything -- student feedback, lesson reminders, my own notes, spontaneous anchor charts. I keep a stash in my desk drawer that I guard carefully.

Pencils. I know the school provides pencils. I know this. And yet. There is always a pencil crisis at the exact wrong moment and I have learned to maintain my own emergency supply. A big bulk pack from Walmart solves this permanently.

Classroom storage bins. These are the backbone of my organizational system. I use clear bins with labels for everything -- supplies, student materials, manipulatives, the various things that accumulate in a classroom over time. Walmart's storage section is genuinely excellent and very budget friendly.

The Things I Buy to Make the Room Feel Like a Place

A small lamp. Overhead fluorescent lighting is not my friend or my students' friend. I have a small lamp in the corner that I turn on during independent reading time and it changes the whole atmosphere. Students notice. A simple lamp from Walmart, done.

Curtains. Not every classroom has windows but mine does and I use them. A set of curtains from Walmart that actually work with the window coverings already there makes the room feel less institutional. Small thing. Big difference.

A few plants. I kill plants with some regularity but I keep trying because the ones that survive make the classroom feel alive and cared for. Low maintenance varieties only. Walmart garden section, late August when things go on clearance.

The Things I've Learned to Wait On

Anything decorative -- bulletin board stuff, themed supplies, seasonal decor -- I wait until it goes on clearance after the back to school rush. Walmart marks this stuff down significantly and it all works just as well in October as it does in August.

The Total

My annual classroom supply spend runs somewhere between $150-200 out of pocket after the school stipend. For some people that feels like a lot. For me it's part of what I do and I've built it into my budget. The Walmart prices make it manageable in a way it wouldn't be otherwise.

If you're a fellow teacher reading this -- I see you. I know you do this too. We don't talk about it enough.

More classroom and back to school posts coming. Thanks for being here.

-- Christin Marie
